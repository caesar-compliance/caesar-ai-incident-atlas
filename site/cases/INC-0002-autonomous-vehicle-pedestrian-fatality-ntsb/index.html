INC-0002

Autonomous vehicle fatally strikes pedestrian during public road testing programme

18 March 2018 Transportation (Autonomous) Severity: Critical Confidence: High FM-SAFEFM-REL
What Happened

According to NTSB Accident Report HWY18MH010, published in November 2019, an autonomous test vehicle struck and fatally injured a pedestrian who was crossing a public road outside a designated crosswalk in Tempe, Arizona, on 18 March 2018. The NTSB investigation found that the vehicle's AI perception system detected an object in the road but repeatedly reclassified it, ultimately failing to initiate emergency braking in time to avoid the collision. The report further found that the safety driver monitoring system had been disabled, and that the safety driver was not attentive at the time of the incident. The NTSB identified the operating company's inadequate safety culture and the failure to implement adequate safety risk assessment procedures as contributing factors.

AI System / Context

Autonomous vehicle perception and control system operating in test mode on a public road. The vehicle was in autonomous mode with a safety driver present at the time of the incident.

Harms
  • Fatal injury to a pedestrian
Impact

A pedestrian fatality resulted. The NTSB investigation contributed to increased regulatory scrutiny of autonomous vehicle testing safety practices and prompted updates to safety guidance for testing programmes. The operating company suspended its public road autonomous testing programme following the incident.

Evidence Required

  • EV-004 — Human oversight record: evidence of safety driver monitoring procedures and their operational status
  • EV-002 — Risk assessment: documented safety case for public road autonomous vehicle testing programmes
  • EV-014 — Post-incident review: NTSB final report and organisational post-incident review records
Governance Lessons
  • Autonomous vehicle testing programmes operating on public roads require robust, independently verified safety cases that account for edge-case perception failures before deployment.
  • Safety driver monitoring systems must remain operational and enforced throughout autonomous vehicle public road testing; their disablement should require explicit safety justification.
  • Pre-deployment testing for autonomous vehicle AI systems should include systematic evaluation of object classification reliability under varied environmental conditions, including low-light and off-crosswalk pedestrian scenarios.
  • Organisations conducting autonomous vehicle testing should maintain an active safety risk assessment process that is reviewed before each public road testing phase.
Affected Stakeholders
  • Pedestrians in autonomous vehicle public road testing zones
  • Autonomous vehicle testing programme operators
  • Regulators overseeing autonomous vehicle testing on public roads

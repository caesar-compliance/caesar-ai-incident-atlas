INC-0012

Federal regulators issue joint guidance warning that AI-based hiring tools may violate disability discrimination protections

12 May 2022 Hiring & Employment Severity: Medium Confidence: High FM-BIASFM-TRANS
What Happened

In May 2022, the US Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) and the US Department of Justice (DOJ) jointly published guidance documents warning that employers using AI-based hiring and assessment tools may be at risk of violating the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA). The EEOC guidance identified specific risk patterns where algorithmic hiring tools could discriminate against individuals with disabilities, including: tools that fail to provide reasonable accommodations for candidates with disabilities during automated assessments; tools that screen out candidates based on characteristics correlated with disability status; and tools that assess abilities not necessary for the job in question. The DOJ companion guidance reinforced that ADA obligations apply regardless of whether an employer uses AI or algorithmic tools in the hiring process, and that employers cannot delegate their non-discrimination obligations to a technology vendor. The joint agency action represents a documented regulatory signal that employers deploying AI-based hiring tools should assess disability discrimination risk and ensure accessibility of automated assessment processes.

AI System / Context

AI-based hiring assessment tools including automated resume screening, video interview analysis, cognitive ability testing platforms, personality assessments, and other algorithmic tools used by employers to evaluate job applicants and employees. These tools may include features that assess physical or cognitive abilities, monitor facial expressions, analyse speech patterns, or score candidate responses using algorithmic models.

Harms
  • Candidates with disabilities may be screened out of hiring processes by AI tools that assess abilities or traits not required for the job, without receiving reasonable accommodations
  • Employers may unknowingly violate ADA obligations by deploying AI hiring tools that have not been evaluated for disability accessibility
  • Candidates may not be informed that AI tools are being used in their assessment or that accommodation options exist for automated evaluation formats
Impact

The EEOC and DOJ joint guidance clarified that the Americans with Disabilities Act applies fully to employer use of AI-based hiring and assessment tools. The guidance identified specific scenarios where algorithmic tools could violate ADA protections — including failure to provide reasonable accommodations during automated assessments, screening out candidates based on disability-correlated characteristics, and assessing abilities not necessary for the job. The joint regulatory action increased compliance exposure for organisations deploying AI hiring tools without documented disability accessibility assessments. This record is distinct from INC-0010 (which addresses EEOC Title VII race/sex discrimination guidance from May 2023); INC-0012 specifically addresses the ADA disability discrimination dimension of AI hiring published one year earlier.

Evidence Required
  • EV-012 — Accessibility and accommodation testing record: evidence that AI hiring tools have been evaluated for accessibility to candidates with disabilities and that reasonable accommodation processes exist
  • EV-002 — Risk assessment: pre-deployment assessment of disability discrimination risk for AI hiring and assessment tools
  • EV-005 — Testing record: evidence that AI hiring tools do not screen out candidates based on characteristics correlated with disability unless job-related and consistent with business necessity
Governance Lessons
  • Employers using AI-based hiring tools retain obligations under disability discrimination law to provide reasonable accommodations during automated assessment processes, including alternative formats or adjusted scoring where the standard tool format disadvantages candidates with disabilities.
  • AI hiring tools that assess abilities, traits, or characteristics not necessary for the essential functions of the target job may disproportionately screen out candidates with disabilities and may violate the ADA unless the assessed characteristics are job-related and consistent with business necessity.
  • Employers cannot delegate ADA non-discrimination obligations to technology vendors; due diligence on whether AI hiring tools have been evaluated for disability accessibility is required before deployment.
  • Organisations deploying AI hiring assessment tools should document accessibility reviews, accommodation request processes, and the job-relatedness justification for each trait or ability assessed by the tool.
Affected Stakeholders
  • Job candidates with disabilities subject to AI-based hiring assessments
  • Employers using AI hiring and assessment tools supplied by third-party vendors
  • AI hiring tool vendors who may share liability for discriminatory tool outputs
  • Disability rights organisations and employment regulators including the EEOC and DOJ

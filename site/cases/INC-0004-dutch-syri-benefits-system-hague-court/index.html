INC-0004

Dutch court finds automated welfare risk-scoring system incompatible with fundamental rights

5 February 2020 Public Sector Severity: High Confidence: High FM-BIASFM-TRANSFM-UNAUTH
What Happened

According to the ruling of the Hague District Court of 5 February 2020 (ECLI:NL:RBDHA:2020:1878), a coalition of civil society organisations brought a legal challenge against the Dutch government's use of the SyRI automated risk-scoring system. The court found that the legal framework governing SyRI was incompatible with Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights, which protects the right to private life. The court found that the system lacked sufficient transparency about the logic by which risk scores were generated, making it impossible for individuals to understand or effectively challenge determinations made about them. The court also found that SyRI had been deployed predominantly in lower-income municipalities, raising concerns about disproportionate impact. The Dutch government did not appeal the ruling and subsequently discontinued the SyRI system.

AI System / Context

Automated risk-scoring system (SyRI — Systeem Risico Indicatie) used by Dutch government authorities to assess the risk of social welfare fraud among benefit recipients. The system combined data from multiple government databases to generate risk scores for individuals.

Harms
  • Individuals subject to automated risk scoring without adequate legal basis or transparency, as found by the court
  • Potential for incorrect fraud-risk classification of welfare recipients in affected municipalities
Impact

The Hague District Court ruled that the legal framework governing SyRI was incompatible with Article 8 ECHR. The Dutch government did not appeal the ruling. The SyRI system was discontinued following the ruling. The case is widely cited in European AI governance literature as an example of automated government decision-support systems being found incompatible with fundamental rights.

  • EV-004 — Human oversight record: evidence of human review procedures before automated risk scores are used to initiate investigations or affect individuals' entitlements
  • EV-014 — Post-incident review: post-ruling review of automated government decision-support systems for compliance with fundamental rights obligations
  • EV-012 — Bias testing record: evidence of demographic impact assessment for automated systems deployed in government welfare contexts
Governance Lessons
  • Automated government systems that assign risk scores to individuals must provide sufficient transparency about the factors and logic used to allow affected individuals to understand and effectively challenge outcomes.
  • AI systems deployed in social welfare contexts require explicit proportionality and fundamental rights impact assessments, with particular attention to disproportionate effects on lower-income or marginalised groups.
  • Government use of automated scoring for fraud detection or eligibility assessment requires a clear, lawful basis and must be consistent with applicable fundamental rights obligations.
  • Organisations should not deploy automated systems that make or inform consequential determinations about individuals without establishing adequate human oversight and review mechanisms.
Affected Stakeholders
  • Social welfare recipients in municipalities where SyRI was deployed
  • Government agencies using automated risk-scoring tools
  • Data protection and fundamental rights authorities

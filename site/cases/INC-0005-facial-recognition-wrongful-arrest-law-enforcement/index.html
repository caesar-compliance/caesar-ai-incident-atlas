INC-0005

Facial recognition misidentification contributes to wrongful arrest of innocent person

9 January 2020 Law Enforcement Severity: High Confidence: High FM-BIASFM-TRANS
What Happened

According to documentation published by the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) and subsequently reported in multiple outlets, in January 2020 the Detroit Police Department arrested an individual on suspicion of a crime based substantially on a match generated by an automated facial recognition system. The individual, who had no connection to the alleged crime, was detained for approximately 30 hours before being released. The ACLU subsequently published documentation of the case and filed legal action on behalf of the individual. The case is consistent with findings documented in the US National Institute of Standards and Technology Face Recognition Vendor Test (NIST FRVT) reports, which identified statistically higher false positive match rates for individuals with darker skin tones across multiple commercial facial recognition systems. The Detroit Police Department subsequently imposed restrictions on the use of facial recognition for arrest decisions. The case is widely cited in AI governance and civil liberties literature as an example of algorithmic bias contributing to a serious harm in a high-stakes law enforcement context.

AI System / Context

Automated facial recognition matching system used by a US municipal police department to identify a suspect from a low-quality surveillance image. The system returned a match that was subsequently used as a basis for arrest without adequate independent corroboration.

Harms
  • Wrongful arrest and detention of an innocent person for approximately 30 hours
  • Psychological harm and reputational impact on the individual and their family
  • Civil rights violation resulting in ACLU civil action
Impact

An innocent individual was wrongfully arrested and detained for approximately 30 hours. The ACLU filed a civil rights action on behalf of the individual. The Detroit Police Department subsequently restricted use of facial recognition for arrest purposes. The case contributed to legislative and regulatory attention on facial recognition use in law enforcement and is widely cited in AI bias and governance literature.

  • EV-004 — Human oversight record: documentation of the corroboration steps required between facial recognition match and arrest decision
  • EV-002 — Risk assessment: pre-deployment risk classification for facial recognition use in law enforcement arrest decisions
  • EV-013 — Incident log: police department records of the arrest decision and the role of the facial recognition match
Governance Lessons
  • Law enforcement use of automated facial recognition matching must require mandatory independent human corroboration before any arrest decision; a facial recognition match alone should not be sufficient basis for arrest.
  • Facial recognition systems deployed in high-stakes law enforcement contexts must undergo independent bias testing across demographic groups — including skin tone and gender — before deployment, and results must be documented.
  • Pre-deployment risk assessments for facial recognition systems in law enforcement must explicitly account for the consequences of false positive matches, including wrongful arrest and detention.
  • Organisations deploying AI-assisted identification systems should maintain clear documentation of the decision pathway between automated output and consequential human action.
Affected Stakeholders
  • Individuals subject to facial recognition-assisted arrest decisions
  • Law enforcement agencies using automated facial recognition systems
  • Civil liberties bodies and regulatory oversight authorities

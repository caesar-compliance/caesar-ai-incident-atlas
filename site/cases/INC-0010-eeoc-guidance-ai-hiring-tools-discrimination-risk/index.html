INC-0010

Regulatory guidance issued on discriminatory risk of AI-based hiring assessment tools under employment discrimination law

18 May 2023 Hiring & Employment Severity: Medium Confidence: Medium FM-BIASFM-TRANS
What Happened

In May 2023, the US Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) published technical assistance guidance addressing the application of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) to employers' use of AI-based hiring and assessment tools. The EEOC guidance stated that employers remain liable under federal employment discrimination law for discriminatory outcomes produced by AI hiring tools, even where the AI system is provided by a third-party vendor, and that vendors may also bear liability. The guidance identified specific risk patterns, including AI systems that produce disparate impact on the basis of race, sex, age, or disability. In the same period, New York City's Local Law 144 — which requires covered employers using automated employment decision tools to conduct and publish independent bias audits — began enforcement. The EEOC guidance and NYC Local Law 144 together represent a documented regulatory response to a pattern of confirmed AI-assisted discriminatory risk in hiring contexts. At time of record creation, no specific EEOC enforcement action arising directly from this guidance had been confirmed as publicly documented.

AI System / Context

AI-based hiring assessment tools used by employers in recruitment and candidate evaluation processes, including automated resume screening, video interview analysis, and structured assessment scoring tools. These tools are used across sectors to evaluate candidates for employment.

Harms
  • Risk of discriminatory employment outcomes for candidates subject to AI-based hiring assessments that produce disparate impact on protected groups
  • Candidates potentially not informed of AI tool use in their evaluation in violation of applicable disclosure requirements
  • Employers exposed to legal liability for AI-assisted discriminatory hiring where adequate bias testing was not conducted
Impact

The EEOC technical assistance guidance clarified that employers and vendors face liability for discriminatory AI hiring tool outputs under existing federal employment discrimination law. NYC Local Law 144 introduced a mandatory independent bias audit requirement for covered automated employment decision tools, with enforcement beginning in 2023. The combined regulatory action increased legal and compliance exposure for organisations deploying AI hiring tools without documented bias assessments. At time of record creation, no specific EEOC enforcement action arising directly from AI hiring tool use had been publicly confirmed; confidence is rated medium accordingly.

Evidence Required
  • EV-012 — Bias testing record: independent bias audit evidence demonstrating that AI hiring tools do not produce disparate impact on protected groups
  • EV-002 — Risk assessment: pre-deployment assessment of discriminatory outcome risk for AI hiring tools across protected characteristics
  • EV-005 — Testing record: validation evidence for AI hiring tools before deployment in consequential employment decisions
Governance Lessons
  • Employers using AI-based hiring assessment tools retain liability under employment discrimination law for discriminatory outputs produced by those tools, including when the tool is supplied by a third-party vendor; due diligence on vendor bias testing is required.
  • AI hiring tools should be evaluated for disparate impact across protected characteristics — including race, sex, age, and disability — before deployment and on an ongoing basis.
  • Candidates subject to AI-based hiring decisions should be informed of the use of automated tools as required by applicable law; transparency about automated assessment is both a legal requirement and a governance best practice.
  • Organisations deploying AI hiring tools should obtain and review independent bias audit results as part of vendor due diligence, and should not rely solely on vendor-provided assurances of fairness.
Affected Stakeholders
  • Job candidates subject to AI-based hiring and assessment tools
  • Employers using AI hiring tools supplied by third-party vendors
  • AI hiring tool vendors
  • Employment regulators including the EEOC and local authorities
